\begin{abstract}
Chess engines rely heavily on heuristic search techniques to cope with the
exponential growth of the game tree. Among these techniques, iterative deepening
combined with alpha-beta pruning has become a standard approach due to its
ability to produce strong moves under strict time constraints.

This report presents the design and implementation of a modular chess engine and
investigates the impact of time constraints on the effectiveness of iterative
deepening search. In particular, the work examines whether limiting the available
search time significantly degrades search quality compared to depth-limited
search, and how well the engine adapts to varying time budgets.

The engine is implemented from scratch in \texttt{C++} using a classical
mailbox-based board representation. It integrates established techniques such as
alpha-beta pruning, transposition tables, quiescence search, heuristic move
ordering, and a two-stage time management system with soft and hard deadlines.

Experimental results indicate that iterative deepening remains robust under
tight time constraints and benefits significantly from improved move ordering
and transposition table reuse. While reduced time budgets naturally limit the
maximum reachable depth, the overall move quality degrades gracefully rather
than catastrophically.

The findings suggest that iterative deepening is well-suited for time-controlled
search scenarios and that even relatively simple engine architectures can
achieve stable and predictable behavior when equipped with appropriate search
heuristics and time management.
\end{abstract}
